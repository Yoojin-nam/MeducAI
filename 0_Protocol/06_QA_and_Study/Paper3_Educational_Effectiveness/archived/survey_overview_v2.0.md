## 1. Purpose of Survey-Based Outcome Measurement

This document provides an overview of all **survey-based outcome measures** used in the MeducAI study, specifically corresponding to **Pipeline-2 (Prospective Observational UX Study)**.

Survey instruments are designed to evaluate the **educational, cognitive, and experiential impact** of MeducAI under real-world radiology board examination preparation conditions.

The surveys aim to capture:

- learners’ perceived cognitive burden,
- motivational and psychological responses,
- satisfaction with AI-assisted learning materials,
- acceptance and trust in AI-based educational tools.

Survey data are interpreted **in conjunction with objective usage logs**, enabling triangulation between subjective experience and actual learning behavior.

---

## 2. Target Population

The survey population consists of:

- radiology residents,
- eligible for the **2026 Korean Radiology Board Examination**,
- who voluntarily provide informed consent for participation.

To preserve **role separation and analytic validity**:

- residents who participated as **expert QA evaluators (Pipeline-1)** or
- residents involved in **content review or adjudication**

are explicitly **excluded** from survey participation.

---

## 3. Survey Timing and Study Phase

Survey administration is aligned with **Pipeline-2 (Track A)** of the MeducAI study design.

### 3.1 Post-Examination Survey (Primary)

The primary survey is administered:

- after completion of the board-style examination or equivalent assessment period,
- following cumulative exposure to MeducAI during exam preparation,
- without intervening in learning strategies or preparation behavior.

This timing is selected to:

- minimize interference with active exam preparation,
- allow reflection on the **overall learning experience** rather than isolated sessions,
- reduce short-term recall bias associated with transient tool usage.

No pre-test or repeated-measures survey is planned, consistent with the **observational study design**.

---

## 4. Core Constructs Measured

The survey captures multiple constructs grounded in educational theory and prior empirical literature. All constructs are **pre-specified** and mapped to corresponding analyses in the Statistical Analysis Plan v2.0.

---

### 4.1 Co-Primary Constructs

#### 4.1.1 Cognitive Load (Co-Primary Construct 1)

Cognitive load is measured using a modified version of the **multi-dimensional Cognitive Load Scale** developed by **Leppink et al. (2013)**.

Subdomains include:

- **Extraneous cognitive load** (co-primary outcome),
- Intrinsic cognitive load,
- Germane cognitive load.

The hypothesis focuses on **reduction of extraneous cognitive load**, reflecting improved instructional design and structural support provided by MeducAI-generated learning materials.

#### 4.1.2 Learning Efficiency (Co-Primary Construct 2)

Learning efficiency captures the **time-related benefit** of using MeducAI compared to traditional study methods.

**Measurement:**
- **Primary item:** "Compared to studying the same material without MeducAI, approximately what percentage of time did you save to achieve the same level of understanding?" (0–100%, continuous)
- **Alternative/Supplemental items:**
  - "How many hours per week did MeducAI save you in study time?" (continuous, hours)
  - "To achieve the same learning outcome, how much faster was your study process with MeducAI?" (1–7 Likert: much slower to much faster)
- **Validation:** Cross-referenced with objective usage logs (total study time) when available

**Rationale:** Directly addresses educational efficiency, a key concern for learners preparing for high-stakes examinations and for educators evaluating educational tools.

#### 4.1.3 Exam Readiness Improvement (Co-Primary Construct 3)

Exam readiness improvement measures **perceived enhancement in board examination preparation confidence** attributable to MeducAI usage.

**Measurement:**
- **Baseline item (pre-intervention):** "How confident are you in your ability to pass the board examination?" (1–7 Likert)
- **Post-intervention items:**
  - "How much has MeducAI improved your confidence in passing the board examination?" (1–7 Likert)
  - "Compared to before using MeducAI, how much better prepared do you feel for the board examination?" (1–7 Likert)
- **Change score:** Post-intervention minus baseline (if baseline available)
- **Alternative:** Single post-intervention assessment if baseline unavailable

**Rationale:** Captures learners' perceived improvement in exam-specific preparation, directly relevant to educational goals and learner motivation.

#### 4.1.4 Knowledge Retention (Co-Primary Construct 4)

Knowledge retention evaluates **long-term retention** of information learned using MeducAI.

**Measurement Options:**

**Option A (Self-reported, Primary):**
- "How well do you feel you retained the information learned using MeducAI compared to information learned through traditional methods?" (1–7 Likert)
- "How confident are you that you will remember the MeducAI-learned material 3 months from now?" (1–7 Likert)

**Option B (Objective, if feasible):**
- Follow-up knowledge assessment 4–6 weeks post-intervention
- Standardized quiz covering topics studied with MeducAI vs. topics studied with traditional methods
- Comparison of retention rates between groups

**Option C (Hybrid, Exploratory):**
- Self-reported retention confidence combined with spaced repetition performance metrics from Anki logs
- Analysis of review intervals and success rates in spaced repetition system

**Rationale:** Addresses long-term learning outcomes, critical for medical education where knowledge retention is essential for clinical practice.

---

### 4.2 Key Secondary Constructs

#### 4.2.1 Academic Self-Efficacy

Academic self-efficacy is assessed using selected items adapted from the **Motivated Strategies for Learning Questionnaire (MSLQ)** (Pintrich & De Groot, 1990).

Items are contextualized to the radiology board examination setting while preserving:

- construct validity,
- directional interpretability,
- compatibility with prior educational research.

#### 4.2.2 Perceived Exam Score Improvement

**Measurement:**
- "How much do you expect MeducAI to improve your actual board examination score?" (1–7 Likert or percentage points, e.g., 0–20%)
- "Compared to not using MeducAI, how many additional points do you expect to score on the board examination?" (continuous, if applicable)

**Rationale:** Provides a concrete, learner-centered outcome measure that directly addresses educational value.

#### 4.2.3 Study Time Efficiency (Objective)

**Measurement:**
- Total study hours logged via MeducAI (from usage logs)
- Self-reported total study time for board examination preparation
- Ratio of MeducAI study time to total study time
- Efficiency metric: Learning outcomes per hour of study time (if outcome measures available)

**Rationale:** Complements self-reported learning efficiency with objective usage data.

---

#### 4.2.4 Learning Satisfaction

Learning satisfaction is measured using items adapted from validated **online learning satisfaction scales** (Eom & Ashill, 2016).

This construct reflects learners' global appraisal of MeducAI as a supportive educational system, rather than satisfaction with individual features or sessions.

---

#### 4.2.5 Technology Acceptance

Technology acceptance is evaluated using constructs derived from the **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)** (Davis, 1989), including:

- perceived usefulness,
- perceived ease of use,
- behavioral intention to use.

These measures assess the **feasibility of sustained adoption** of AI-assisted educational tools in residency-level medical education.

---

#### 4.2.6 Trust in AI-Generated Educational Content

Trust in AI-generated educational materials is assessed using custom-developed items grounded in prior literature on:

- automation bias,
- human–AI interaction,
- AI-assisted clinical decision support.

This construct is included to:

- contextualize observed learning outcomes,
- detect potential over-reliance or excessive skepticism toward AI-generated content.

---

### 4.3 Behavioral and Emotional Covariates

To account for real-world confounding factors common in residency training, the survey includes items assessing:

- learning-related stress,
- sleep quality,
- mood stability,
- regular physical activity.

These variables are treated as **covariates** in multivariable statistical models, not as primary outcomes.

---

## 5. Relationship to Usage Logs

Survey responses are linked to **anonymized MeducAI usage logs**, including:

- total study time,
- frequency of review and spaced repetition,
- interaction counts with Anki cards.

This linkage enables:

- dose–response analyses,
- interpretation of subjective experience in relation to actual engagement,
- sensitivity analyses distinguishing perception from exposure intensity.

---

## 6. Statistical Role of Survey Data

Survey-derived variables are used as:

- **primary and secondary outcomes** (Pipeline-2),
- covariates in adjusted regression models,
- exploratory variables for subgroup and dose–response analyses.

All scale scoring rules, composite scores, and analytic roles are **pre-specified** in the Statistical Analysis Plan v2.0 to minimize analytic flexibility.

---

## 7. Documentation and Version Control

- Each survey instrument is versioned (e.g., v1.0).
- Survey content is fixed prior to data collection.
- Instruments are cross-referenced with IRB-approved materials.

Detailed survey items, scoring rules, and administration procedures are documented in dedicated subdirectories and are not regenerated post hoc.

---

## Summary

The MeducAI survey framework is designed to rigorously evaluate **learner experience, cognitive burden, and acceptance of AI-assisted educational support** under authentic board examination preparation conditions.

By combining validated educational scales, contextualized adaptations, behavioral covariates, and objective usage logs, Pipeline-2 provides a clinically meaningful and methodologically defensible assessment of MeducAI’s educational impact.

---

**Note on Objective Examination Scores:** While actual board examination scores are not included as primary outcomes (due to heterogeneous examination conditions and timing constraints), the study now includes **perceived exam score improvement** and **exam readiness improvement** as co-primary outcomes. These measures capture learners' expectations and confidence, which are educationally meaningful and directly influenced by educational support tools. Future studies may incorporate objective examination scores if standardized assessments become available.
